## I. Introduction: The Morning After the Rebellion

The first two papers have traveled a path: the first diagnosed the functional transformation of the means of cognitive production: how compute, data, and model weights each transformed across three vectors from functional resources into control variables, producing a three-class structure, where hyper-concentration constitutes a pro tanto injustice and cognitive exclusion escalates into a violation of basic rights. The second paper examined the action logic of the dispossessed, demonstrated the dead end of individual technological exit, the limits of liberal action theory under deep capture, and formalized the **cognitive coordination trap**: a structure of multiple binding constraints composed of spatial dispersion, infrastructure capture, no-trigger co-optation, and identity opacity. Resistance strategies were organized into two tiers: strategies within the structure (data contribution, community fine-tuning, serf competition) face insurmountable ceilings; alternative production entities (publicly or community-owned public AI infrastructure) point toward changing the property rights structure itself.

But the end of this path, collective action breaking the old centralized structure of the means of cognitive production, is only another starting point. What comes after the breaking?

This is a question that no theory of structural change can evade, and it is the most difficult question in history. Breaking an unjust structure is relatively easy; when historical tensions accumulate to a certain threshold, the structure cracks on its own. What is difficult is: after the crack, how to ensure that the new structure does not repeat the same mistakes?

This question is not hypothetical. It carries a heavy historical record.

## II. The Paradox of Revolution: New Masters, Old Structures

### 2.1 The Lesson of the Soviet Union (Revisited)

The third paper of the Wealth Trilogy has already discussed the lesson of the Soviet Union in detail. That lesson applies equally here and merits restating.

The largest-scale revolutionary experiment of the twentieth century ended with the Soviet Union. The capitalists and landlords of Tsarist Russia were overthrown, and property was "nationalized." But Party bureaucrats replaced the old capitalists, seizing control over the power to allocate resources. They did not need nominal ownership, because they controlled the power to distribute resources itself. The Soviet Union did not eliminate hyper-concentration of holdings; it merely converted hyper-concentration from the form of private capital into the form of bureaucratic power. The function of wealth did not change. It remained a variable for controlling the feasible sets of others; only the names of the controllers changed.

### 2.2 The Structural Replication Risk of the Means of Cognitive Production

The risk revealed by the Soviet case, that "revolution replaces people but does not automatically replace structures," takes on distinctive forms in the context of the means of cognitive production.

Suppose a social movement successfully breaks the current compute lords' centralized control over the means of cognitive production. The old AI giants are broken up or nationalized, cognitive serfs gain better conditions, and the excluded gain access. Then what?

If the new institutions do not structurally prevent the re-concentration of the means of cognitive production, new concentration will re-emerge within a generation. It may take the following forms:

**The alienation of nationalization**. If the means of cognitive production are nationalized, but the state itself is captured by a new bureaucratic class, then concentration has merely shifted from private capital to state bureaucratic power. The new managers of state-owned AI enterprises may not need nominal ownership, because they control the power over compute allocation, data access, and model deployment. This is structurally isomorphic to the Soviet Union's entry point shifting from "capital" to "Party membership": the entry point for the means of cognitive production shifts from "the market" to "the administrative apparatus."

**The emergence of new oligarchs**. If no dispersive mechanisms are embedded after breaking the old structure, market logic will re-manufacture concentration. Even if old AI giants are broken up, economies of scale (the physical economies of scale in compute, the self-reinforcement of data flywheels) will drive new players toward new concentration. Within a generation, new compute lords will re-emerge, only under different names.

**Re-concentration through international asymmetry**. Even if one country successfully disperses the means of cognitive production domestically, international power dynamics may re-manufacture concentration. If one country's AI industry is dispersed and weakened, while another country's compute lords continue to concentrate and thereby gain capability advantages, the former may, under competitive pressure, move back toward concentration. This is a dimension in which the means of cognitive production differs from traditional wealth; it carries strong geopolitical implications.

If the diagnosis of the first paper holds (that the problem lies in the mechanism of functional transformation of the means of cognitive production itself, rather than in the specific entities that possess these means), then replacing entities does not by itself automatically solve the problem. You can break up every current AI giant, but if the new institutions allow the means of cognitive production to re-concentrate deep into the functional transformation zone, new compute lords will emerge within a generation. The disease of the structure does not heal merely because the patients have been replaced.

### 2.3 Revolution Is Not the End Point

This means: revolution is not the end point. Breaking the old structure is only the first step, and not the most difficult one. The most difficult step is: how to design a new structure that makes the re-concentration of the means of cognitive production difficult, that makes institutional capture costly, and that preserves the dispossessed's capacity for continuous intervention.

The second paper argued that solidarity and collective action are necessary conditions for changing the structure. Solidarity is likewise a necessary condition for maintaining the new structure: Institutional design is not a one-time project; it is a structure that requires continuous maintenance, and the force for that maintenance remains solidarity.

## III. Preventing Re-Phase Transition: Core Issues of Institutional Design

### 3.1 Defining the Objective

The objective of new institutions is not to eliminate AI enterprises. This is neither possible (any complex society requires mechanisms for organizing and accumulating the means of cognitive production) nor desirable (AI as a cognitive tool is not inherently unjust; the injustice diagnosed in the first paper occurs only after the functional transformation deepens, not before it).

The objective is: **to prevent the means of cognitive production from sliding from "functional resources" into "control variables."**

More specifically, the new institutions must operate on three progressive layers: preventing holdings of the means of cognitive production from penetrating deep into the functional transformation zone (that is, from shifting from means of training and inference into variables that alter the feasible sets of others), equipping institutions with sufficient anti-capture mechanisms to make attempts to cross the threshold difficult to succeed, and preserving the capacity for self-correction when capture partially occurs.

These three progressive layers (prevention, resistance, correction) are of a piece with the institutional design framework of the third paper of the Wealth Trilogy. The only difference is: the Wealth Trilogy's objects of constraint were natural person wealth and legal person wealth, whereas this paper's objects of constraint are the three vectors of the means of cognitive production. The first paper already demonstrated that the phase transition occurs independently on each of the three vectors; therefore, constraints must symmetrically cover all three. Under existing conditions, any constraint targeting only a single vector can hardly interrupt the phase transition mechanism itself.

### 3.2 Why Taxation and Antitrust Alone Are Insufficient

The first paper deliberately deferred discussion of policy instruments. But at the level of institutional design, a question must be confronted directly: why are traditional instruments (taxation, antitrust) insufficient to prevent the re-phase transition of the means of cognitive production?

**Taxation is a downstream instrument.** Progressive taxation is an ex post adjustment of excess profits; it intervenes only after algorithmic rents have already been monopolized. But the first paper demonstrated the self-reinforcing cycle of institutional capture: once the means of cognitive production are concentrated to the point of influencing rule-making, holders can rewrite the tax code itself. The strength of taxation depends on the will of the legislature, and the legislature can be captured. Moreover, AI enterprises possess abundant means of profit shifting (cross-border pricing, intellectual property licensing fees, R&D deductions), creating a substantial gap between nominal tax rates and actual tax burdens. Taxation is one tool in the toolbox, but it is a downstream tool, not an upstream one.

**Traditional antitrust is insufficient to address the triple phase transition.** Traditional antitrust targets single-dimensional market power (market share in a particular product market). But the phase transition of the means of cognitive production spans three vectors, and single-dimensional antitrust cannot cover it. Even if antitrust breaks up an AI giant's model business, its compute infrastructure, data flywheel, and ecosystem lock-in may persist and re-manufacture concentration in new forms. Moreover, traditional antitrust's "consumer welfare standard" (using price as the primary criterion) cannot capture phase transitions under free inference: free services do not raise consumer prices, but they manufacture deeper dependency through co-optation and data extraction. This means the evaluative framework of traditional antitrust itself must be updated to address the forms of phase transition in the AI era.

Upstream instruments change the **generation structure** of the means of cognitive production (who obtains what share of compute, data, and weights under what conditions), rather than merely engaging in ex post redistribution. The Three-Principle Framework of this paper is precisely an upstream instrument.

### 3.3 The Three-Principle Framework

To address the three vectors, the new institutions require a design of three principles operating in parallel. What this paper proposes are **principles**, not a **policy menu**. Principles are normative requirements for institutional arrangements; each principle can have multiple modes of implementation. The choice of specific policy options depends on empirical context and cannot be determined once and for all at the normative level. Principles are different: they are normative requirements derived from the diagnosis of the first paper, their number determined by the complementary bottleneck structure of the three vectors (Section 2.2 of the first paper demonstrated the necessity and joint sufficiency of the three vectors), while the modes of implementation under each principle are open, comparable, and adjustable to context.

**Compute Principle**: No single entity should control sufficient compute to unilaterally set the frontier of cognitive capability. This principle addresses the compute phase transition: when compute crosses the threshold, it transforms from a functional resource into a control variable, enabling its holder to unilaterally determine who can enter the race. The normative requirement of the principle is: control over compute must be sufficiently dispersed that no single entity can unilaterally set the frontier.

Modes of implementing this principle include but are not limited to: (a) public compute as a public utility; (b) compute antitrust and break-up; (c) a GPU tax or compute excess profits tax, directing rents toward public purposes; (d) transparency requirements and concentration monitoring for compute usage. These modes each have their own merits and drawbacks and can be implemented individually or in combination; their relationship is that of **comparable substitutes** rather than mutually exclusive options. The position of this paper is: **so long as a given arrangement satisfies the normative requirement that "no single entity unilaterally sets the frontier," it is a legitimate implementation of the Compute Principle.** Which specific mode is chosen depends on context.

**Data Principle**: The data flywheel should not become a mechanism by which a single entity locks out competitors and users. This principle addresses the data phase transition: when data accumulation crosses the critical scale, the self-reinforcing flywheel makes it impossible for competitors lacking an equivalent user base to catch up. The normative requirement of the principle is: control over data should correspond to generative contribution, and data should not be monopolized by a single entity as a lock-out tool.

Modes of implementing this principle include but are not limited to: (a) data trusts, in which trustees manage rights and interests on behalf of data contributors; (b) public data pools, providing alternatives to commercial flywheels; (c) data portability and interoperability requirements, reducing user lock-in; (d) Data Dividend or data profit-sharing, compensating data contributors. Again, these modes are comparable substitutes.

**Weight Principle**: Models trained using public data should give back to the public in some form. This principle addresses the weight phase transition: when closed weights become a tool of ecosystem control, the holder exploits public data (web-crawled corpora, publicly funded research, open-source community contributions) without giving back. The normative requirement of the principle is: model outputs that utilize public inputs should not be fully privatized.

Modes of implementing this principle include but are not limited to: (a) mandatory weight disclosure (requiring models trained using public data to release their weights); (b) tiered disclosure (opening fine-tuning rights, periodically releasing older weights, allowing independent auditing); (c) an AI License framework (analogous to voluntary licensing mechanisms such as drug patent pools); (d) open-source incentives (tax or regulatory benefits for enterprises that disclose weights). These modes are likewise comparable substitutes.

The three principles operate in parallel and mutually support one another. Reform of a single principle will be hedged by violations of the others (the first paper demonstrated that the three functional transformations mutually reinforce one another). Only when all three principles are simultaneously satisfied can the self-reinforcing closed loop of concentration be structurally dismantled. However, the **weighting** among the three principles and the **choice of implementation modes** are open. This paper does not prescribe that "the Compute Principle must be realized through public compute" or "the Weight Principle must be realized through mandatory disclosure"; it prescribes only the principles themselves. This preserves normative determinacy alongside operational flexibility.

**A clarification regarding whether the principles conflict.** A possible concern is: when the Compute Principle and the Weight Principle pull in different directions in specific policy contexts, how is the conflict adjudicated? This paper's response is: the three principles do **not in principle conflict** at the normative level, because they operate on different vectors (three independent nodes of the complementary bottleneck). The Compute Principle governs the dispersion of compute control; the Weight Principle governs the public return of model outputs; their domains of operation do not overlap. What a reviewer might construct as a "conflict" (e.g., "an enterprise trades weight disclosure for a compute concentration exemption") is a **bargaining scenario in policy implementation**, not a normative conflict at the level of principle. In a bargaining scenario, the principles say that both normative requirements should be satisfied, while the proposed bargain satisfies only one. This does not constitute a conflict between principles, but a gap between the principles and partial compliance. Trade-offs in specific policy implementation (e.g., "how high should the GPU tax rate be," "how long should the weight disclosure lag be") are empirical questions, dependent on context, and do not require the principle framework to provide a priori adjudication rules.

## IV. Implementation of the Compute Principle

### 4.1 Design Rationale

The root of the compute phase transition is economies of scale: large-scale compute clusters have lower unit costs and therefore tend naturally toward concentration. Preventing the compute phase transition cannot deny the physical fact of economies of scale, but can, through institutional arrangements, prevent compute control from being monopolized by a small number of entities.

**Public compute as a public utility.** By analogy with roads, electricity, and water, frontier compute can be classified as infrastructure with public utility characteristics. The state or public entities build and operate large-scale compute clusters, making them available to researchers, startups, and public institutions at cost or at regulated prices. This provides an alternative outside the compute lords, enabling actors who lack the capital to purchase private compute to participate in frontier model training and research.

**Compute antitrust and break-up.** Enforce antitrust against private compute providers that exceed a specified market share, limiting any single entity from controlling more than a certain proportion of national (or global) compute capacity. This is not about eliminating private compute, but about preventing private compute from concentrating deep into the functional transformation zone.

**Transparency requirements for compute usage.** Require large compute providers to disclose their compute usage (who rented how much compute, when, and for what purpose), enabling continuous monitoring of compute concentration. This is the data foundation for phase transition monitoring (Section VII).

### 4.2 Historical Reference: Public Utility Regulation

The publicization of compute is not without historical precedent. Infrastructure characterized by economies of scale, electricity, railways, telecommunications, has, in different countries and at different times, undergone publicization or public utility regulation.

The U.S. electricity industry underwent a transition from private monopoly to regulated public utility in the early twentieth century. States established public service commissions to regulate electricity companies' pricing, service quality, and investment plans. This model is not perfect (regulatory capture has occurred repeatedly), but it prevented electricity, an infrastructure with strong economies of scale, from being entirely reduced to a tool of private monopoly, instead serving the public within a regulated framework.

The telecommunications industry provides another reference. Many countries historically nationalized or heavily regulated telecommunications networks to ensure universal service, so that even residents of remote areas could obtain basic telecommunications services. The counterpart of the universal service principle in the AI context is **universal cognitive service**: ensuring that even marginal language communities and low-income groups can obtain basic AI services. Public compute can serve as the material foundation for universal cognitive service.

### 4.3 Limitations and Challenges

**High material cost.** Building and operating frontier compute clusters requires enormous investment: billions of dollars in hardware procurement, sustained energy supply, and professional operations teams. Public compute is not a cheap solution. The state's fiscal capacity determines the upper bound of public compute scale, and fiscally constrained states (especially in the Global South) may be unable to afford it. This means that compute publicization is more feasible in developed countries, while in developing countries it may require international cooperation (e.g., regional public compute pools).

**Physical constraints of economies of scale.** Even with public compute, the economies of scale of private compute persist. If a given private compute provider's scale far exceeds that of public compute (because it can continuously invest capital in expansion), public compute may be merely a marginal substitute rather than a genuine competitive force. For public compute to truly constitute a check on compute lords, it must reach a scale at least comparable to the largest private player, which is extremely challenging both fiscally and politically.

**The catch-up pressure of technological iteration.** Compute technology (chip architectures, interconnect technologies, cooling solutions) iterates rapidly. Once public compute is built, it may quickly become technologically obsolete relative to the latest private compute. This means public compute requires sustained investment in technological upgrades; otherwise, it will degrade from a "substitute" into an "obsolete artifact."

**The intervention of geopolitics.** The supply chain for frontier compute (especially high-end GPUs) is highly concentrated in a small number of countries. A country's capacity to build public compute is constrained by whether it can obtain high-end chips, which in turn is constrained by geopolitics (e.g., U.S. chip export controls). This means compute publicization is not only a domestic policy issue but also a geopolitical one. Public compute construction in the Global South may be limited by the political control of chip supply.

## V. Implementation of the Data Principle

### 5.1 Design Rationale

The root of the data phase transition is the self-reinforcement of the data flywheel: more users → more data → better models → more users. To prevent the data phase transition, the mechanism by which "data is monopolized by a single entity for its flywheel" must be severed.

**Data trusts.** Establish legal entities (data trusts) in which trustees manage data rights and interests on behalf of data contributors (users). Users contribute data to the trust, and the trust, under fiduciary duty, negotiates data usage terms with AI enterprises on behalf of users' interests, obtains data compensation, and supervises data usage. The trust does not "return" data to individual users (individual data has very low value in isolation); rather, it concentrates dispersed data rights into a collective force capable of bargaining. This is structurally isomorphic in spirit to the "universalization of beneficiary rights" discussed in the third paper of the Wealth Trilogy: preserving the existing property rights framework while embedding collective distribution mechanisms at the level of benefit flows.

**Public data pools.** Establish publicly funded open datasets (covering multiple languages and domains) as alternatives to commercial datasets. Any researcher, startup, or public institution can use public data pools to train models, thereby weakening the monopolistic force of commercial data flywheels. The quality of public data pools depends on sustained public investment and community contribution; they connect directly to the data contribution strategy in the first tier of the second paper.

**Data portability and interoperability requirements.** Require AI enterprises to legally permit users to export their data (including usage history, preference feedback, fine-tuning data) in standard formats and migrate it across different platforms. This reduces user lock-in, enabling users to "vote with their feet," not formally (Section II already demonstrated that formal foot-voting is ineffective under lock-in effects), but substantively (when data can be ported, the cost of migration is significantly reduced).

### 5.2 Historical Reference: Prototypes of Data Trusts and Precedents for Public Data Pools

Data trusts remain at present theoretical prototypes and preliminary experiments, but there are some referable precedents.

The UK's Open Data Institute (founded by Tim Berners-Lee and Nigel Shadbolt) has been promoting the concept of data trusts and has conducted preliminary experiments in domains such as healthcare and urban governance. These experiments indicate that data trusts are feasible in specific domains (e.g., medical data), but for cross-domain, large-scale general-purpose AI data, the governance structure of the trust (who serves as trustee, how to balance the interests of different contributors, how to negotiate with AI enterprises) remains an open legal design question.

Precedents for public data pools include Common Crawl (open web-crawled corpora), Wikipedia (a human-edited open knowledge base), and Common Voice (an open speech dataset). The existence of these public data pools demonstrates that collective contribution can produce valuable public alternatives. But their scale and quality currently fall far short of commercial AI enterprises' private data. Common Crawl's corpus, though enormous, cannot match the quality and organization of carefully cleaned and labeled commercial data. For public data pools to become a genuine check on commercial flywheels, sustained, institutionalized public investment is required, not merely voluntary contribution.

### 5.3 Limitations and Challenges

**The epistemological problem of data valuation.** Section VII of the first paper already identified the fundamental difficulty of data valuation for operationalizing $\rho_{ARA}$: how is the value of different types of data contributions to be assessed? For a data trust to allocate compensation, it must answer this question, and any valuation scheme implicitly embeds normative judgments. This means the governance structure of data trusts must include democratized valuation procedures, rather than having values determined unilaterally by AI enterprises or technical experts.

**The difficulty of collective action.** The effectiveness of a data trust depends on a sufficient number of users delegating their data rights to the trust. But this is itself a collective action problem (Section IV of the second paper): each user has an incentive to "free-ride": let others join the trust, let the trust bargain with AI enterprises for better terms, and enjoy the improved terms without joining oneself. If enough users choose not to join, the trust lacks sufficient bargaining power. This difficulty is structurally isomorphic with all collective action dilemmas and requires an organizational foundation (user consciousness-building, the visibility and credibility of the trust) to overcome.

**The absence of transnational governance.** Data flows across borders, and AI enterprises operate transnationally. A data trust established by one country may face bargaining counterparts (transnational AI enterprises) that lie outside the jurisdiction of that country's laws. The effective operation of data trusts requires a transnational governance framework, and such a framework is currently almost nonexistent.

**The tension between privacy and sharing.** There is a tension between data sharing (especially behavioral data) and privacy protection. Data trusts must find a balance between "making data usable" and "protecting individual privacy." Technically, methods such as differential privacy and federated learning can partially mitigate this tension (allowing models to learn without directly accessing raw data), but they introduce additional complexity and performance costs.

## VI. Implementation of the Weight Principle

### 6.1 Design Rationale

The weight phase transition is the most distinctive of the three phase transitions, because its correction cost is the lowest (as argued in Section 3.3 of the first paper: the zero marginal reproduction cost of weights makes "disclosing weights" technically almost costless). This distinctiveness makes the Weight Principle the most politically difficult (because it directly threatens the core rent source of compute lords) but the most technically feasible.

**Conditional disclosure.** The core design is: **to require that models trained using public data disclose their weights in some form.** The qualifier "using public data" is the key limitation: if a model's training uses entirely private data (data that is enterprise-owned, legally acquired, and non-public), disclosure is not required. But if a model's training uses public data (web-crawled public corpora, publicly funded research results, datasets contributed by the open-source community), then the model's weights should be made available to the public in some form.

The jurisprudential basis for this requirement is Murphy and Nagel's argument: property rights are institutional artifacts, not natural rights. An AI enterprise's "ownership" of model weights is not a natural right but an institutional right conferred by the legal framework. When the exercise of this right (closing weights to extract excess rents) conflicts with social interests (models trained using public data should give back to the public), the legal framework has the authority to redraw the boundaries of the right. The closure of weights for models trained using public data is not "protecting private property" but "privatizing the output of public inputs," which is jurisprudentially challengeable.

**Disclosure can be tiered in form.** Full disclosure (directly releasing weights) is one form, but not the only form. Intermediate forms worth considering include: opening fine-tuning interfaces (allowing third parties to fine-tune on a base model even without releasing full weights), periodically releasing old weights that are N generations behind in capability (when the frontier model updates to a new generation, the previous generation's weights are disclosed), and weight auditing (not disclosing weights but allowing independent third-party auditing of model performance, bias, and safety properties).

**"Tiered" weight disclosure.** Different disclosure requirements can be set according to the model's use case and impact. Models used for critical public services (education, healthcare, law) face the highest disclosure requirements; models used for general commercial purposes face lower requirements; models used for internal R&D are not required to disclose. This tiered approach makes disclosure requirements proportional to the model's social impact.

### 6.2 Historical References: Open-Source Software and Compulsory Drug Licensing

Model weight disclosure has two historical references, each providing both inspiration and caution.

**The open-source software movement.** Software (especially its source code) likewise has a marginal reproduction cost close to zero. The open-source software movement (exemplified by GNU/Linux, Apache, Firefox) demonstrated that, even without mandatory requirements, open source can form an ecosystem that competes with closed-source commercial software in the software domain. The existence of open-source software broke the complete concentration of the software domain and provided alternatives. But it must be noted: the success of open-source software did not eliminate closed-source commercial software (Microsoft and Oracle remain enormous); it merely provided a countervailing force. The goal of model weight disclosure is likewise not to eliminate closed-source models, but to provide a countervailing force.

The open-source software movement also offers a caution: the maintenance of open-source projects depends heavily on voluntary labor and limited funding, and their sustainability is a persistent problem ("maintainer burnout" is widespread in the open-source community). If, after model weights are disclosed, the continuous improvement of open-source models lacks institutionalized support, open-source models will gradually fall behind closed-source models with ample resources for continuous training, ultimately losing their countervailing force.

**Compulsory licensing of drugs.** After a drug's molecular formula is disclosed (patent expiration or compulsory licensing), generic drugs can be produced at far lower prices than the original patented drug. This is a direct precedent for "disclosure reducing rents." The World Trade Organization's TRIPS Agreement permits member states to implement compulsory licensing for drugs in public health emergencies. This mechanism demonstrates that, even within a capitalist framework, when private intellectual property rights seriously conflict with social interests, the law can mandatorily alter the exclusivity of intellectual property.

Compulsory drug licensing also provides a cautionary lesson, one that has a precise counterpart in the AI domain and must be directly addressed in the design of the Weight Principle. Originator pharmaceutical companies frequently use **"evergreening" strategies**: applying for new patents on minor modifications to drugs, extending the effective patent protection period, in order to evade compulsory licensing or patent expiration. The **Novartis Glivec case** is the clearest precedent: Novartis applied for a patent on a new crystalline form (the beta crystalline form of imatinib mesylate) of the known compound imatinib, in order to extend its monopoly on this anti-cancer drug. The Indian Patent Office rejected the application in 2006, and the Supreme Court of India upheld the rejection in 2013, relying on Section 3(d) of the Indian Patent Act—a provision specifically designed to prevent evergreening, requiring that new forms of known substances must demonstrate "significantly enhanced therapeutic efficacy" to be patentable.

The AI domain has a structurally isomorphic evergreening risk. Even if the Weight Principle requires that "models trained using public data disclose their weights," enterprises can evade this in the following ways: applying for new "version rights" or technical patents on every fine-tuned version, aligned version, or RLHF iteration of a model, such that although old weights are disclosed, the latest capability version actually deployed remains perpetually closed. Or through the continuous patenting of model architectures, training methods, and data processing pipelines, even if weights are disclosed, rents can be maintained through intellectual property claims on other links in the chain. This follows the same logic as the first paper's demonstration that "the three functional transformations mutually reinforce one another, and breaking a single link is hedged by the other links."

Therefore, the implementation of the Weight Principle **must be accompanied by an anti-evergreening mechanism.** The design of this mechanism can draw on the spirit of Section 3(d) of the Indian Patent Act: new versions of known models (fine-tuned versions, aligned versions, iterated versions) do not constitute new grounds for closure unless they demonstrate a "significant capability leap" (rather than incremental improvement). Disclosure obligations should cover the version actually deployed, not only cover already-obsoleted old versions. Periodic disclosure (e.g., "disclosure of old weights that are N generations behind") is meaningful only if the lag time is such that the disclosed version still retains ecological relevance; if what is disclosed is always a version already eliminated by the market, "disclosure" is nominal.

It must be acknowledged that the substantive standard of "significant capability leap" is more difficult to objectify in the AI domain than "significant therapeutic efficacy" in the pharmaceutical domain. The high manipulability of AI benchmarks is a known problem. But the core force of the anti-evergreening mechanism comes not from a perfect substantive standard, but from the **reversal of the burden of proof**: the enterprise must affirmatively demonstrate that its new version has a significant improvement (rather than incremental iteration), otherwise it does not automatically obtain new grounds for closure. This is consistent with the effectiveness logic of Section 3(d) of the Indian Patent Act: its force lies in reversing the burden of proof, not in providing an uncontroversial efficacy standard. The specific threshold for "significant capability leap" is an open problem to be resolved, but the procedural mechanism of reversing the burden of proof is operable and sufficient to significantly raise the cost of evergreening evasion.

### 6.3 Limitations and Challenges

**The greatest political resistance.** Weight disclosure directly threatens the core rent source of compute lords, and will therefore encounter the strongest political resistance. Compute lords will mobilize all the channels of institutional capture diagnosed in Section V of the first paper to resist disclosure: lobbying (opposing disclosure legislation in the name of "protecting intellectual property"), opinion-shaping (manufacturing panic in the name of "open source is dangerous"), and talent capture (buying off researchers who might promote disclosure with high salaries). The zero reproduction cost of weights makes disclosure technically almost costless, but precisely for this reason, the forces opposing disclosure will concentrate entirely at the political level. Making the Weight Principle the most difficult to implement, but also the one whose structural impact, once achieved, is the greatest.

**The weaponization of the "safety" narrative.** The narrative most likely to be used to oppose weight disclosure is "safety": "disclosing frontier model weights is dangerous because malicious actors could use them to generate disinformation, launch cyberattacks, or manufacture bioweapons." This narrative has some truth to it (frontier models do carry risks of misuse), but it is also extremely easy to weaponize: compute lords can oppose all disclosure in the name of "safety," even when the benefits of disclosure (breaking the phase transition, raising $\rho_{ARA}$) far outweigh the risks.

The key to addressing this challenge is to distinguish **genuine safety concerns** from **rent protection disguised as safety**. One possible distinguishing criterion is: if an enterprise simultaneously claims that "weights cannot be disclosed (because of safety)" and "we can continue to commercially deploy this model (because it is safe)," then its safety narrative contains a deep internal tension: if the model is safe enough to commercially deploy, it is safe enough to disclose weights; if the model is dangerous enough that weights cannot be disclosed, it is dangerous enough that it cannot be commercially deployed. This logical test can be used to identify which safety narratives are sincere and which are disguises for rent protection.

This concern is not theoretical speculation; it already has empirical manifestations. Meta Chief AI Scientist Yann LeCun has publicly accused the leaders of OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Anthropic of stoking AI fear to advance regulatory capture, manufacturing a narrative that "AI is too dangerous to be developed by anyone but a small number of trusted large enterprises," thereby providing justification for restricting open-source and small competitors. Sam Altman's 2023 testimony before the U.S. Senate calling for a licensing regime for frontier models was criticized by open-source advocates as creating market entry barriers for startups and the open-source community in the name of safety. Academic analysis has also begun to systematically examine this phenomenon ("AI safety and regulatory capture" has become a searchable research topic). Meanwhile, according to public reports, OpenAI and Anthropic's lobbying expenditures increased substantially in 2025. This empirical pattern, leading enterprises simultaneously emphasizing safety risks loudly, calling for higher regulatory thresholds, and increasing lobbying investment, is highly consistent with the classic pattern of regulatory capture in history.

**International competitive pressure.** If one country unilaterally requires weight disclosure while others do not, the former's AI enterprises may be at a disadvantage in international competition (because they are forced to disclose core assets that competitors can retain). This creates a "unilateral disarmament" dilemma for weight disclosure legislation. Resolving this dilemma requires international coordination, analogous to "common but differentiated responsibilities" in climate negotiations, AI governance may need some form of international framework to coordinate disclosure requirements across countries and avoid a "race to the bottom." But achieving international coordination is itself difficult and time-consuming.

## VII. Operationalizing the Algorithmic Rent Allocation Ratio: Control Variable and Evaluation Metric

$\rho_{ARA}$, introduced in the first paper, is operationalized in this paper from a normative anchor into concrete policy instruments. Its two new functions are: a control variable for policy intervention and an evaluation metric for institutional performance.

### 7.1 As a Control Variable for Policy Intervention

$\rho_{ARA}$ can be used to evaluate and rank different policy instruments: **to what extent can a given policy instrument raise $\rho_{ARA}$?**

- Data rights legislation raises the numerator by requiring data compensation: if legislation requires data contributors to receive x% of AI enterprise revenue as compensation, $\rho_{ARA}$ rises by approximately x percentage points.
- A public AI fund (funded by AI enterprise taxation) directs a portion of rents to public purposes: if a tax of y% on AI enterprise profits is levied and injected into a public fund for low-resource language support, public compute, and open-source maintenance, $\rho_{ARA}$ rises by approximately y percentage points.
- Model weight disclosure requirements reduce the monopolized portion of the denominator, subjecting the excess rents of closed weights to competition or publicization.
- Compute antitrust directly reduces individual lords' rent extraction capacity; low-resource language data ratio requirements ensure that the excluded are at least included in distribution at the data level.

These instruments need not be implemented simultaneously. The value of $\rho_{ARA}$ lies in providing a unified metric for evaluating any single policy instrument. This enables different policies to be compared and ranked rather than operating in isolation. It must be acknowledged that this paper's operationalization of $\rho_{ARA}$ is directional; "raising the numerator raises the ratio" is logically straightforward. A more analytically powerful operationalization should be able to answer comparative static questions: how does $\rho_{ARA}$ evolve over time under unchanged policy? How do the marginal impacts of different policy instruments on $\rho_{ARA}$ differ (data trust vs. public compute vs. weight disclosure, which has the greatest marginal impact)? These questions require quantitative empirical modeling of AI enterprises' cost structures, data valuation, and rent flows, which exceeds the scope of this paper as a normative framework and is an important direction for subsequent research.

### 7.2 As an Evaluation Metric for Institutional Performance

$\rho_{ARA}$ can also serve as a continuous metric for evaluating the performance of the new institutions. If the new institutions successfully prevent the re-phase transition of the means of cognitive production, $\rho_{ARA}$ should exhibit an upward trend (or at least not decline). If $\rho_{ARA}$ begins to decline under the new institutions, this is an early warning signal: indicating that the phase transition may be reoccurring and that the institutions' anti-capture mechanisms may be eroding.

This requires establishing a mechanism for the regular measurement and public reporting of $\rho_{ARA}$. This is itself an institutional design problem: who measures it? How are measurement methods standardized? How is data obtained (especially internal data that AI enterprises are unwilling to disclose)? One possible design is to establish an independent "Algorithmic Rent Audit Agency" (analogous to a financial audit agency), responsible for regularly evaluating and publicly reporting $\rho_{ARA}$ for major AI economies. The independence of this agency (its freedom from capture by compute lords) is the precondition for its effectiveness, which returns us to the core problem of anti-capture (Section VIII).

### 7.3 $\rho_{ARA}$ Is Not a Target Value

It must be emphasized that $\rho_{ARA}$ is not a target value that must reach 1. Complete distribution of rents is neither possible nor desirable in reality (incentives for innovation and investment need to be preserved). It is a directional guide: the current $\rho_{ARA}$ is likely too low, and raising it is a requirement of justice; but the magnitude, speed, and instrumental mix of the increase depend on the institutional endowments and political possibilities of specific societies.

A rough international comparative reference: at the height of the welfare state (Northern Europe in the mid-to-late twentieth century), the "rent redistribution ratio" of the traditional economy (the proportion of total economic surplus redistributed through wages, taxation, and welfare) may have been in the range of 0.4–0.6. If the AI economy's $\rho_{ARA}$ could be raised from its current extremely low level to the 0.2–0.3 range, that would already be an enormous structural improvement: it would mean at least one-fifth to one-third of algorithmic rents are returned to co-producers, rather than being almost entirely monopolized. But this is only a heuristic reference, not a normative target value. The normative criterion of judgment is Rawlsian: whether the distribution improves the interests of the least advantaged (cognitive serfs and the excluded).

## VIII. The Paradox of Anti-Capture

The Three-Principle Framework (Compute Principle, Data Principle, Weight Principle), together with the operationalization of $\rho_{ARA}$, constitutes a set of institutional frameworks for preventing the re-phase transition of the means of cognitive production. But this paper must confront directly a core paradox, one already identified in the third paper of the Wealth Trilogy: **any anti-capture mechanism can itself be captured.**

### 8.1 The Guardian Paradox

Who executes the anti-capture mechanisms? The managers of public compute, the trustees of data trusts, the regulators of weight disclosure requirements, the auditors of $\rho_{ARA}$: these are all "guardians." Their duty is to prevent the re-concentration of the means of cognitive production. But guardians themselves are human; they can be bought, coerced, or captured.

The managers of public compute may be lobbied by compute lords to allocate public compute preferentially to research projects affiliated with compute lords, rather than to genuinely independent public research. The trustees of data trusts may be bought off by AI enterprises, making concessions in negotiations that harm data contributors. The regulators of weight disclosure requirements may be captured by compute lords, granting exemptions in the name of "safety" to models that should be disclosed. The auditors of $\rho_{ARA}$ may be pressured to underestimate the algorithmic rent share of compute lords.

This means: the design of anti-capture mechanisms cannot rely on the premise that "there exists a neutral, uncaptured executing agent." That is a fragile premise. In reality, every executing agent faces the risk of capture.

**A corroborating case from the history of public utility regulation.** The U.S. Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935 (PUHCA) is a classic precedent of anti-concentration institutional design. At the time, approximately **86%** of operating electric utilities belonged to interstate holding company pyramid systems; PUHCA forcibly broke up these structures, confining them to single integrated systems and placing them under SEC oversight. This institution operated for approximately 70 years, partially succeeding in curbing excessive concentration in the electricity industry. But the **Energy Policy Act of 2005 repealed PUHCA**, leading to a resurgence of merger and acquisition activity in the electricity and gas industries, reversing decades of anti-concentration policy. The rise and fall of PUHCA is a textbook case of "the anti-concentration institution itself being captured": an institution that was once effective was ultimately abolished by the very industry it was supposed to constrain, under the pressure of sustained industry lobbying and political realignment.

The specific mechanisms of PUHCA's failure deserve analysis, because they have direct implications for the vulnerabilities of the Three-Principle Framework. PUHCA's repeal was not sudden but the result of decades of gradual erosion: the industry accumulated political influence through sustained lobbying; under the ideological shift toward neoliberalism in the 1990s, "deregulation" acquired cross-partisan political legitimacy; and it was finally formally abolished in the energy legislation of 2005. PUHCA's weakness lay in its reliance on a **single executing agent** (the SEC) and a **single legal framework** (federal securities law). Once that agent and framework were captured by political forces, the entire institution lost its guardian. This analysis precisely supports what Section 8.2 of this paper will argue: the principle of **multiple redundancy**. If an anti-concentration institution does not rely on a single guardian but is instead composed of multiple mutually independent layers of execution (legal requirements, administrative regulation, judicial review, independent auditing, citizen participation), then capture requires simultaneously buying off all layers, and its cost is far higher than capturing a single agent. The lesson of PUHCA is not "institutions inevitably fail" but "institutions with a single guardian are more likely to fail than institutions with multiple redundancy."

The life cycle of an institution is not infinite merely because it was once successful; it can be repealed, hollowed out, or reinterpreted as an instrument for protecting vested interests. The implication of this historical precedent for this paper's Three-Principle Framework is clear: **what institutional design strives for is not a permanent solution, but time: time for the dispossessed to accumulate organizational capacity, time for society to maintain monitoring consciousness.**

**A corroborating contrast from data governance.** The same institutional idea can produce diametrically opposite outcomes under different governance details. Finland's **Findata** (established in 2019 under the Act on the Secondary Use of Health and Social Data), operating as a one-stop data licensing agency, has been relatively successful under a governance structure of statutory mandate, independent institutional operation, and a clear list of purposes, and continues to operate under the European Health Data Space (EHDS) framework. The UK's **NHS care.data** program (launched in 2013), based on a similar centralized data governance idea, failed due to inadequate public communication, insufficient informed consent mechanisms, and weak governance, leading to **over 1 million patients opting out**; the program was formally abolished in 2016. This contrast has direct implications for the implementation of the Data Principle: data trustification does not automatically work; governance details, the clarity of the statutory mandate, the independence of trustees, the construction of public trust, determine the success or failure of the institution. The normative requirement of the principle ("the data flywheel should not become a lock-out mechanism") is determinate, but the institutional forms for realizing this requirement must be context-sensitive.

### 8.2 Multiple Redundancy: Confronting Structure with Structure

The only structural means of addressing the guardian paradox is **multiple redundancy**: not relying on a single executing agent or a single anti-capture mechanism, but establishing multiple mutually independent, mutually supervising layers of anti-capture.

**Multiple redundancy at the compute layer**: public compute has not one manager but multiple independent public compute institutions (national, state/provincial, academic consortium levels), which compete with and supervise one another. If one is captured, the others can serve as alternatives.

**Multiple redundancy at the data layer**: there is not one data trust but multiple trusts representing different groups (users, researchers, marginal language communities), which bargain with AI enterprises on different dimensions. The buy-off of one trust does not collapse the entire data governance.

**Multiple redundancy at the weight layer**: weight disclosure is enforced not by a single regulatory agency, but by multiple complementary mechanisms: legal requirements, administrative regulation, judicial review, independent auditing, and sustained pressure from the open-source community.

**Multiple redundancy for $\rho_{ARA}$**: algorithmic rent auditing is performed not by a single institution, but by official auditing agencies, academic researchers, and civil society organizations' independent estimates, mutually cross-validating one another.

The cost of multiple redundancy is complexity and expense. But it is the only structural means of confronting the guardian paradox: **not pinning hope on any single guardian remaining uncaptured, but making capture require the simultaneous buy-off of enough independent agents, thereby raising the cost of capture to an unaffordable level.**

This is consistent with the principle of Federalist No. 51, cited in the third paper of the Wealth Trilogy: "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Anti-capture does not rely on eliminating ambition but on using ambition to check ambition, using the mutual checks of multiple independent agents to ensure that the capture of any single agent is insufficient to dissolve the entire framework.

### 8.3 Continuous Intervention by the Dispossessed

Multiple redundancy reduces the probability of successful capture, but it cannot eliminate capture. The ultimate anti-capture force comes from the continuous intervention of the dispossessed themselves. If cognitive serfs and the excluded possess institutionalized channels of participation (a voice in public compute allocation, voting rights in data trust governance, citizen initiative rights for weight disclosure requirements), then any attempt to capture the anti-capture mechanism will face resistance from the dispossessed.

This means that the new institutions cannot merely be institutions "designed for the dispossessed" (designed by elites, executed by elites, with the dispossessed as passive beneficiaries); they must be institutions "in which the dispossessed participate in design and management." The institutionalized participation of the dispossessed is the last line of defense of the anti-capture framework.

But here we return to the core difficulty of the second paper: the organizational capacity of the dispossessed (especially the cognitively excluded) is weak. If the dispossessed cannot organize effectively, their institutionalized participation channels may become nominal: nominally having a voice, but substantively dominated by more powerful interest groups. This constitutes a cycle: effective anti-capture requires the organization of the dispossessed; the organization of the dispossessed requires institutionalized space; institutionalized space requires the protection of the anti-capture framework. This cycle has no starting point; it must begin from some imperfect present, accumulating gradually.

## IX. Re-examining Historical Cases

Having proposed the Three-Principle Framework, it is necessary to honestly test it: are there comparable attempts in history? What were their successes and failures?

### 9.1 Open-Source Software: Partial Success as a Countervailing Force

The open-source software movement is the closest historical precedent for the publicization of the means of cognitive production. It demonstrated that, under conditions of near-zero reproduction cost (software), public-good alternatives can emerge without compulsion and constitute a countervailing force against commercial concentration.

The success factors of open-source software include: legal infrastructure (the legal force of open-source licenses such as the GPL), organizational vehicles (nonprofit organizations such as the Linux Foundation and Apache Foundation), corporate participation (companies like IBM and Google finding it advantageous to support open source), and community culture (the hacker ethic and voluntary contribution). These factors have together made open-source software a force not to be ignored in the software domain.

But open-source software also has its limitations: it has not eliminated the concentration of commercial software (Microsoft and Oracle remain enormous); its sustainability depends on sustained voluntary and corporate investment (maintainer burnout is a real problem); and in certain domains (such as enterprise-grade databases, professional design software), it still cannot compete with commercial products. The lesson of these limitations for the AI domain is: open-source models can serve as a countervailing force, but they cannot dissolve concentration on their own. They need to synergize with the institutional realization of the Compute Principle, the Data Principle, and the Weight Principle to form genuine structural checks.

### 9.2 Public Broadcasting and Universal Service: Precedents for Publicization

Public broadcasting (e.g., the UK's BBC, Japan's NHK) is another reference. Public broadcasting, supported by public funds (license fees or taxation) and oriented toward public service (rather than commercial profit), provides information public goods outside commercial media. Its existence means the information ecosystem is not entirely commercialized; the public has at least one information source not dominated by the interests of advertisers and shareholders.

The success of public broadcasting lies in: it demonstrates that public funds can sustainably support a form of cognitive production not entirely dominated by commercial logic. Its limitations lie in: it depends on the political will of the state (public broadcasting budgets are frequently subject to political pressure); its independence requires institutionalized protection (the BBC's independence structure is a complex legal and political design); and it may be at a resource disadvantage when facing competition from global commercial media. These experiences have direct lessons for the design of public compute: public compute requires a similar independence structure (insulated from short-term political pressure) and a sustainable funding mechanism.

The universal service principle in telecommunications is also a precedent: even in unprofitable areas, basic telecommunications services must be guaranteed. The counterpart of this principle in the AI context is **universal cognitive service**: even in unprofitable language communities and regions, basic AI services must be guaranteed. Public compute can serve as the material foundation for universal cognitive service.

### 9.3 The Drug Access Movement: A Political Precedent for Rent Redistribution

The drug access movement (especially the HIV/AIDS drug access movement of the 2000s) is a political precedent for breaking intellectual property monopolies. At the time, the high prices of antiretroviral drugs made them unaffordable for AIDS patients in developing countries. A global social movement (a coalition of patient groups, NGOs, developing country governments, and some generic drug companies) promoted compulsory licensing and generic production of drugs. The annual cost of antiretroviral therapy dropped from approximately $12,000–$15,000 to approximately $100, enabling millions of patients in developing countries to receive treatment.

The lesson of this movement is: **monopoly rents on intellectual property can be broken, but this requires cross-border social movements and political coalitions.** Market forces alone or states alone are insufficient; what is needed is a coalition of patients (the dispossessed), NGOs (external solidarity actors), developing country governments (political force), and alternative suppliers (generic drug companies / open-source models). The lesson for the AI domain is: breaking the concentration of the means of cognitive production may require a similar transnational coalition: a union of cognitive serfs, digital rights NGOs, Global South states, and the open-source community.

The drug access movement also provides a caution: originator pharmaceutical companies use "evergreening" strategies (applying for new patents on minor modifications) to continuously evade compulsory licensing. The AI domain may witness similar "evergreening," with enterprises continuously patenting model architectures, training methods, and data processing, maintaining rents through the closure of other links even when weights are disclosed. This means the three principles must mutually support one another; no single principle can be treated as "already resolved."

## X. Conclusion: Maintenance Without End

### 10.1 The Logical Chain of the Three Papers

The three papers form a complete logical chain, corresponding precisely to the Wealth Phase Transition Trilogy:

**First Paper (Diagnosis)**: The phase transition of the means of cognitive production deprives hyper-concentration of moral protection. Compute, data, and model weights each transform, on three vectors, from functional resources into control variables; the triple phase transition mutually reinforces, forming a self-reinforcing closed loop, producing a three-class structure; institutional capture tilts the rules; cognitive exclusion escalates injustice into a violation of basic rights. $\rho_{ARA}$ is introduced as the linking variable between diagnosis and norm.

**Second Paper (Action)**: Individual technological exit is a dead end. Liberal action theory suffers diminishing efficacy under deep capture. The core contribution of this paper is the formalization of the **cognitive coordination trap**: a structure of multiple binding constraints composed of spatial dispersion, infrastructure capture, no-trigger co-optation, and identity opacity. The first two components have been addressed in platform labor studies (Vallas & Schor, Prassl); the CCT's incremental contribution lies in identifying the distinctive constraints of AI annotation labor in two dimensions: employer visibility (obscured by the outsourcing chain) and leverage points (tasks globally re-allocatable). Resistance strategies are organized into two tiers: strategies within the structure (data contribution, community fine-tuning, serf competition) face insurmountable ceilings; alternative production entities point toward changing the property rights structure itself, and their institutional design is precisely the theme of the third paper. Ultimate institutional change requires collective political action and solidarity.

**Third Paper (Design)**: The lesson of the Soviet Union is that revolution replaced the people but left the structure intact. Since the cause lies in the phase transition mechanism of the means of cognitive production, the remedy must also be structural. The **Three-Principle Framework**, Compute Principle, Data Principle, Weight Principle, symmetrically covers the three complementary bottlenecks; the normative requirement of each principle is determinate, while the modes of implementation under each are open and comparable. $\rho_{ARA}$ is operationalized as a policy control variable and a performance evaluation metric. The Weight Principle is accompanied by an anti-evergreening mechanism. Yet guardians themselves may be captured; multiple redundancy and the continuous intervention of the dispossessed are structural means of anti-capture, but no permanent solution exists.

This chain is not a circle. A circle returns to the origin; this chain is an upward spiral. Each iteration operates at a higher level: the first paper's diagnosis is completed within the framework of mainstream political economy, using the diagnostic tools of industrial organization and public choice to show that the means of cognitive production have deviated from the norms of distributive justice. The second paper's action turns to the logic of collective action after the failure of mainstream frameworks. The third paper's design asks, after collective action has broken the old structure, how the new structure is to be maintained. If the spiral continues, the next round of diagnosis will confront a more complex question: where the new institutions have deviated from the new norms, and the new norms themselves must be established in practice and cannot be given a priori.

### 10.2 Return to Action

The end point of this logical chain returns to the starting point: action.

The structure is unjust. Change requires solidarity. The new structure requires continuous maintenance, and maintenance, in turn, requires solidarity.

This is not circular reasoning; it is the dialectic of structures. There is no once-and-for-all solution. The mechanism of functional transformation of the means of cognitive production diagnosed in the first paper will not disappear because of a single institutional design; it will re-emerge in new forms within the new structure. The logic of solidarity argued in the second paper will not become obsolete because of the establishment of the new structure; it must still operate within the new structure, only shifting from "breaking the old structure" to "maintaining the new structure."

Solidarity under the new structure differs substantively from solidarity under the old structure. Solidarity under the old structure was bare-handed: no institutional tools, no organizational experience, no monitoring capacity. Solidarity under the new structure gains three things: institutional tools (legal oversight channels, statutory participation rights, accountability mechanisms for public compute), organizational experience (the organizational capacity accumulated through the operation of data trusts and public compute), and monitoring capacity (the early warning provided by regular auditing of $\rho_{ARA}$). These differences mean that the solidarity that maintains the new structure is not simple repetition but an upgrade: from "the force that breaks the structure" to "the immune system that maintains the structure."

Maintenance without end. Justice without completion. Only a continuous process: diagnosis, action, design, re-diagnosis, re-action, re-design.

This is not pessimism; it is honesty. The three papers have traveled a path: from diagnosis to action, from action to design, from design back to action. This path has no end. Public compute may be captured; data trusts may be bought off; weight disclosure may be evaded in the name of "safety"; the auditing of $\rho_{ARA}$ may be pressured. But public compute may also not be captured; data trusts may persist in operation; weight disclosure may, under sufficient social pressure, be implemented; $\rho_{ARA}$ may become a widely adopted governance tool. Which direction concretely prevails is not determined in theory but in struggle. What theory can do is point struggle toward a directionally correct exit. The road out of that exit must be walked by the actors themselves.

Structural justice cannot be declared "complete" on any given day; it is a responsibility that each generation must assume anew. The first two papers argued why action is necessary; this paper has argued what is needed after action. But the three papers together point ultimately in one direction: **continuously, collectively, and in an organized manner, resist the re-concentration of the means of cognitive production; continuously, collectively, and in an organized manner, raise $\rho_{ARA}$; continuously, collectively, and in an organized manner, maintain the justice of cognitive infrastructure.**

This is not a choice; it is a condition. So long as the mechanism of concentration of the means of cognitive production exists, this condition will not change.
