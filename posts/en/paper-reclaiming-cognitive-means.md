## I. Introduction: After the Diagnosis

The previous paper completed a diagnostic task. It argued for the functional transformation of the means of cognitive production: when compute, data, and model weights enter the functional transformation interval, their primary use shifts from tools of training and inference to control variables that constrain others' feasible sets; this transformation occurs independently on each of the three carriers and mutually reinforces itself; it creates a three-class structure; hyper-concentration distorts rules through institutional capture, constituting pro tanto injustice; and when that distortion reaches the point of cognitive exclusion, the injustice escalates into a violation of fundamental rights. The diagnostic chain closes here.

The diagnosis deliberately stopped at diagnosis. The previous paper did not prescribe, did not dispense medicine, did not prescribe policy instruments. That was correct. Diagnosis has the honesty of diagnosis; conflating diagnosis with prescription only renders both ambiguous.

But the question after diagnosis cannot be evaded. **What are the dispossessed to do?**

If the hyper-concentration of the means of cognitive production has lost moral protection (if the argument of the previous paper holds), then is it legitimate to reclaim from this concentration the conditions for participating in cognitive production? If legitimate, how can it be prevented from degenerating into disorganized technological Luddism? If illegitimate, or if legitimate but impracticable, then where lies the way out for the dispossessed?

These questions push this paper from the theoretical space of the previous paper into another space. Once action is touched upon, all the tools used in the previous paper (industrial organization economics, public choice theory, Rawlsian distributive justice) begin to falter. They can diagnose the injustice of institutions, but under the premise that "institutions have been deeply captured," their capacity to guide "how the dispossessed should act" rapidly diminishes.

This paper acknowledges this and switches discourse. This switch follows the same argumentative structure as the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy, *The Legitimacy of Reclamation and the Necessity of Solidarity*: the previous paper completed its critique within mainstream frameworks; this paper departs from the boundary of mainstream frameworks and moves toward narratives of collective action and class analysis: not because this narrative is more "correct," but because it is the intellectual tradition that has most systematically developed the proposition of "how the oppressed change structures through collective action." Yet this paper also faces an additional difficulty absent from the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy: **the dispossessed in the AI era are harder to organize than traditional workers.** This difficulty is one of the central problems of this paper.

## II. The Legitimacy of Technological Exit and Its Dead End

### 2.1 Legitimacy: Derived from the Diagnosis

First, grant the premise: the dispossessed have the right to refuse participation in an unjust structure.

This right follows directly from the diagnosis of the previous paper. If the hyper-concentration of the means of cognitive production already constitutes pro tanto injustice (because the monopoly extraction of algorithmic rents seizes the surplus of co-production, because institutional capture distorts rules, because cognitive exclusion deprives people of fundamental participatory rights), then refusing this structure is not equivalent to ordinary "breach of contract" or "free-riding." Logically, refusal of a structure that has lost moral protection does not constitute moral fault. Just as, against a regime that has lost legitimacy, resistance does not constitute rebellion.

In the AI context, this refusal can take many forms: refusing to use a particular closed-source model, refusing to contribute data, refusing to build applications on a particular base, turning to open-source alternatives, or even withdrawing from AI use. These actions all have legitimacy derived logically from the diagnosis of the previous paper.

### 2.2 Dead End: The Structural Ineffectiveness of Individual Exit

The problem is not with the logic. The problem is with practice.

**Individual technological exit** (one person or a small group refusing to use a particular AI service) faces a triple dilemma in practice.

**First dilemma: asymmetric costs of exit.** The previous paper argued that free inference is a core tool of incorporation: it eliminates the price barrier to use, making refusal irrational. A cognitive serf who refuses mainstream AI pays the price of losing the cognitive capacity gains with which their peers compete. In a market where AI capability increasingly becomes a prerequisite for employment, education, and information access, the exit-er does not "preserve purity" but "gets eliminated by competition." Compute lords lose nothing from the exit of individual serfs, while the exit-er loses their living space. This cost asymmetry makes large-scale individual exit practically impossible: each person, after rational calculation, will choose to continue using, even knowing that such use structurally incorporates them.

**Second dilemma: exit does not touch the structure.** Even if some individual somehow bore the cost of exit, their exit changes nothing. Compute lords' compute remains concentrated, the data flywheel keeps spinning, closed-source weights remain sealed, the three-class structure stands unchanged. Individual exit is a gesture expressed within an unjust structure, but the structure itself does not budge. This aligns with the argument of the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy on individual reclamation: **it does not change the structure; it merely redistributes a few (individual) positions within an unjust structure.** It is a symptom, not a cure.

**Third dilemma: exit intensifies marginalization.** Most cruelly, the cost of individual exit is borne primarily by the most vulnerable groups. A cognitive serf with abundant resources (highly skilled, with savings, with alternatives) might be able to bear the cost of exiting a closed-source ecosystem; but a barely-sustained cognitive serf, or a cognitively excluded person, simply cannot afford any form of exit. They struggle even to participate, let alone exit. Thus, individual exit as a "strategy of resistance" is in fact only open to a small privileged group. Not only does it fail to mobilize the dispossessed, it actually intensifies stratification among the dispossessed: those who can exit do so, those who cannot are forced to stay, and the gap between them widens further.

### 2.3 The Modern Variant of Luddism

It is worth cautioning that if individual technological exit takes collective form (e.g., a "refuse AI" social movement), it would degenerate into a modern variant of Luddism.

The nineteenth-century Luddites smashed looms because they correctly identified the machines as instruments that deprived them of their livelihood. But smashing machines does not change capitalist relations of production: capitalists can buy new machines, while Luddite workers were hanged or exiled. The tragedy of the Luddites lies not in their anger lacking justification (their anger was justified), but in their directing their attack at technology rather than structure. They smashed the symptom, not the cause.

"Machine-smashing" in the AI era would take new forms: refusing to use AI, demonizing AI, demanding bans on AI, attacking AI infrastructure. These actions may have strong emotional legitimacy (their anger comes from real dispossession), but they are structurally ineffective: compute lords will not abandon the concentration of the means of cognitive production because some people refuse AI, just as nineteenth-century capitalists did not abandon the factory system because Luddites smashed looms. Worse, the "refuse AI" movement can be exploited by compute lords: they can use it to portray opponents as "anti-progress," "anti-innovation" Luddites, thereby consolidating in public discourse the narrative that "AI is inevitably dominated by a few large corporations."

The correct direction is not to reject technology, but to change the property rights structure of technology. As argued in the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy: **the nature of the problem does not permit resolution at the individual level, nor at the disorganized level. It demands resolution at the structural level.** Both individual technological exit and Luddism are dead ends, and their very existence proves precisely this point.

## III. The Boundaries of Liberal Theories of Action

This is not to say that liberalism has no theory of action; on the contrary, it has a rich tradition. Yet under the premise that "AI infrastructure has been deeply captured," the efficacy of these theories of action is structurally limited.

### 3.1 The Liberal Action Landscape in the AI Context

Liberalism has a rich landscape of action in the AI domain:

**Consumer choice and market competition.** The classic liberal answer: if a particular AI provider behaves improperly, users can vote with their feet and switch to alternatives. Market competition will punish misconduct and reward good behavior.

**Innovation competition.** The Schumpeterian answer: even if concentration currently exists, disruptive innovation can break the existing order. Today's compute lords may be tomorrow's disrupted, just as IBM was disrupted by Microsoft, and Microsoft by Google.

**Regulatory reform.** Antitrust enforcement, data protection legislation, AI safety regulation. These institutional tools can constrain the behavior of compute lords and protect the rights of users and competitors.

**Open-source community self-governance.** The Ostromian answer: communities can manage common-pool resources through self-governance rules without state intervention. Open-source communities (Hugging Face, EleutherAI, LAION) are exactly such instances of self-governance, providing alternatives beyond the compute lords.

**Technical standards and interoperability.** By establishing open technical standards (unified model formats, open API standards, interoperable inference frameworks), the cost of migrating between different bases can be reduced, thereby weakening any single lord's lock-in power.

Denying the existence of these paths would be dishonest. The problem is not that they do not exist, but the premises on which each depends.

### 3.2 The Premises of Each Path and Their Failure Conditions

**Consumer choice fails under lock-in effects.** When data flywheels make a particular model the de facto standard, users cannot migrate even if they want to, because of the quality gap. Section III of the previous paper argued how data phase transitions manufacture user lock-in: the cost of migration is not "switching accounts," but "accepting a significant capability downgrade." In a market where AI capability is increasingly critical, accepting a capability downgrade means accepting competitive disadvantage. Thus, consumer choice in markets with strong lock-in effects is formally present but substantively ineffective: you "can" choose to leave, but the cost of doing so renders the choice itself meaningless.

**Innovation competition fails under the kill zone.** The "kill zone" research cited in the previous paper shows that players with overwhelming compute and data advantages can rapidly replicate any startup's innovation, killing it before it establishes market position. This means that the narrative of "disruptive innovation breaking concentration" faces severe challenges under the current AI industrial structure: not that innovation is impossible, but that it is very difficult for innovation to survive once it occurs. The heuristic power of historical analogies (IBM to Microsoft to Google) lies in the fact that barriers to entry in that era were far lower than today: training a useful software product did not require billions of dollars in compute, whereas training a frontier AI model does. The magnitude difference in barriers to entry greatly diminishes the force of historical analogies.

**Regulatory reform fails under institutional capture.** This is the core diagnosis of Section V of the previous paper: compute lords, through lobbying, revolving doors, and setting high compliance thresholds in the name of "safety," have captured the process of AI regulatory legislation. A captured regulatory body cannot effectively constrain its captors. The Wealth Trilogy has already detailed the self-reinforcing cycle of institutional capture, and this cycle operates with the same logic in the AI domain. Regulatory reform is not ineffective (it can improve certain local conditions), but under conditions of deep capture, it is insufficient to change the structure that produces the capture.

**Open-source community self-governance fails under the scale asymmetry.** Ostrom's self-governance model applies to local, limited-scale common-pool resource management. But when confronting global-scale AI infrastructure concentration, open-source communities are severely mismatched with compute lords in scale and resources. Open-source communities rely on voluntary labor and limited funding; compute lords command tens of billions of dollars in R&D budgets. Open source can provide alternatives (this is important; it is a condition for resistance), but it cannot dismantle concentration on its own, because the compute scale required to train frontier models exceeds the capacity of any open-source community.

**Technical standards fail under ecosystem binding.** Open standards can theoretically reduce migration costs, but in practice, compute lords have strong incentives to resist open standards (because open standards weaken their lock-in power). They will promote their own proprietary standards, maintaining user dependence on their bases through ecosystem tool binding, API incompatibility, and non-transferability of fine-tuning assets. Open standards can only be established when multiple parties with sufficient power jointly push for them, and the formation of such multi-party coalitions is itself a collective action problem.

### 3.3 The Shared Premise of Liberal Theories of Action

The failure of the above paths has a common premise-level cause: **they all presuppose an institutional environment that has not been deeply captured.** Consumer choice presupposes that markets are competitive (no lock-in), innovation competition presupposes that barriers to entry are surmountable (no kill zone), regulatory reform presupposes that regulators are independent (not captured), open-source self-governance presupposes parity of scale (no compute chasm), and technical standards presuppose the willingness of multiple parties to cooperate (no ecosystem binding).

The diagnosis of the previous paper precisely challenges these presuppositions: when the concentration of the means of cognitive production reaches a systemic degree (when the phase transitions of compute, data, and weights have all occurred, when institutional capture spans multiple channels including legislation, regulation, public discourse, talent, and standards), the explanatory power and efficacy of these theories of action rapidly decline. They can improve conditions, but they are insufficient to change the structure that produces the conditions.

Liberal theories of action are powerful under the presupposition of institutional neutrality. When this presupposition does not hold (when institutions themselves are part of the problem), its theories of action reach their boundary. Diagnosis is its strength; action guidance under conditions of deep capture is not. This conclusion follows the same argumentative structure as the judgment of liberal theories of action in the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy, the only difference being that the carrier has shifted from traditional wealth to the means of cognitive production.

## IV. The Cognitive Coordination Trap

Liberal theories of action lose efficacy under deep capture. But there is an even more fundamental question here: why don't the dispossessed unite? The reason is not that they do not think the system is problematic, but that unity itself faces a structural dilemma.

**Relationship to existing platform labor research.** The argument of this section builds on an existing research tradition. Vallas & Schor (2020) have already identified the general organizational difficulties of platform labor. Platforms render the labor process more "legible" to employers than to workers, enabling employers to "actively discriminate against their workforce". Prassl (2018) has systematically analyzed the collective rights dilemmas of platform workers. The repression/channeling mechanism in Tilly's political process model, and Earl's (2022) research on digital repression, both provide mature frameworks for understanding the suppression of collective action in platform contexts. This paper does not claim that all four components below are original; "spatial dispersion" and "infrastructure capture" have already been articulated in platform labor studies. The incremental contribution of this paper lies in identifying the specifically intensified difficulties that AI annotation workers face, compared to the general gig economy, in the two dimensions of **employer visibility** and **leverage points**, and integrating these difficulties into a nameable, testable structural mechanism.

This section formalizes this as the Cognitive Coordination Trap (CCT). The trap consists of four mutually constraining components, of which the first two (spatial dispersion, infrastructure capture) are general features of platform labor, and the latter two (incorporation without trigger points, identity opacity) are structurally intensified in AI annotation labor. The four together constitute a structure of **multiple binding constraints**: when multiple constraints bind simultaneously, relaxing any single one does not improve the outcome.

### 4.1 The Classic Paradox of Collective Action (Preliminary)

Olson revealed a counterintuitive truth: when a group's interests are harmed, each individual in the group has an incentive to wait for others to take the lead while free-riding on the result. Granovetter's threshold model further deepens this dilemma: each person has a "threshold" for joining collective action, and if even the person with the lowest threshold is unwilling to go first, the entire chain never starts.

These are classic dilemmas of collective action, applicable to all dispossessed groups. But classic theory implicitly assumes one premise: the dispossessed **can be organized**, they just **choose not to organize**. The solution of the threshold model is "someone must go first." Once someone breaks the silence, the chain begins. The history of traditional labor movements seems to confirm this: union organizers step forward, workers follow, strikes spread, institutions respond.

in the AI era, this implicit premise has been shaken. The problem is no longer that "the dispossessed choose not to organize," but that "**the organizational conditions that would allow the dispossessed to unite are precisely eliminated by the concentration structure of the means of cognitive production.**" This is not an intensified version of the classic coordination dilemma, but a structurally different dilemma: a trap.

### 4.2 Definition and Four Components of the Trap

**Definition of the Cognitive Coordination Trap (CCT):** In the cognitive economy, collective action faces a structure of multiple binding constraints: the organizational conditions that would allow the dispossessed to unite (physical concentration, autonomous infrastructure, visible trigger points, shared identity) are weakened or eliminated by the platform-mediated labor structure (this applies generally to the gig economy), and are further intensified in AI annotation labor by two specific dimensions: the obscuring of employer visibility (multiple layers of outsourcing chains mean no single "concrete employer" can be pointed at) and the loss of leverage points (annotation tasks can be globally reallocated, so regional work stoppages cannot halt production). The trap consists of four mutually constraining components.

**Component I: Spatial dispersion.**

Traditional labor movements were possible because workers were **physically concentrated** in factories. Workers in the same factory worked in the same space every day, knew each other, had daily interactions, and shared labor experiences. This physical concentration provided the organizational foundation for collective action: unions could establish branches in factories, and strikes could spread from one workshop. The organizational strength of nineteenth- and twentieth-century labor movements derived in large part from a paradoxical fact: capital's concentration of workers in production sites actually created conditions for workers' collective action.

Cognitive serfs lack this condition. Their "workplaces" are dispersed: each uses AI services before their own computer, builds applications in their own company, contributes data in their own life. A developer using GPT-4 in Beijing, a developer using the same model in Bangalore, and a developer using the same model in Berlin have no daily interaction, no shared space, no visible shared identity among them. The dispersion of the lowest stratum of cognitive serfs (data annotation workers) is even more extreme: each works at a network node in their own home, not even knowing who else is annotating similar tasks for the same platform.

**Component II: Infrastructure capture.**

Collective action requires infrastructure for communication and organization. Traditional labor movements relied on union halls, workers' newspapers, and assembly spaces. Infrastructure that, while politically suppressed, was not physically controlled by employers.

In the AI era, the primary infrastructure that cognitive serfs can use for organization, digital platforms, communication tools, social networks, **is precisely in the hands of the targets of collective action (or their allies).** You cannot use the platforms of compute lords to organize actions against compute lords. This is not only a technical problem but a structural asymmetry of power: the tools of organization are themselves a link in the chain of capture. Even if platforms do not actively censor (and in most cases they do not, because the scale of resistance is too small to warrant it), their visibility control over organizing actions (algorithmic recommendation, content moderation) itself constitutes a latent constraint. Annotation workers coordinate in WhatsApp groups, but these groups run on the cloud services of compute lords, using communication infrastructure funded by compute lords.

**Component III: Incorporation without trigger points.**

Traditional labor movements were often triggered by visible oppressive events: layoffs, pay cuts, workplace injuries. These events provided the emotional fuel and moral legitimacy for collective action: "they cut our wages by 20%" is a powerful rallying cry because it points to a concrete, visible, attributable injustice.

The "dispossession" of cognitive serfs lacks such visible trigger points. Free inference is not oppression; it is a service. Data contribution is not expropriation; it is usage. Ecosystem lock-in is not imprisonment; it is convenience. Every service provided by compute lords improves the immediate condition of cognitive serfs (more efficient, more capable), even as those same services simultaneously consolidate structural dependence. This paradox of "being dispossessed while being improved" makes resistance difficult to gain traction for emotional mobilization. Annotation workers do have visible trauma (psychological trauma, extreme low wages), but their trauma is diluted across the four layers of the outsourcing chain (worker—contractor—platform—end client) to the point where no one claims responsibility; no single "concrete employer" can be pointed at, protested against, or pressured.

**Component IV: Identity opacity.**

Collective action requires a shared identity ("we are workers"). But "cognitive serf" is currently not a widely recognized identity. Most AI users see themselves as "consumers" or "users," not as "serfs." The absence of shared identity means that the collective "we" cannot be constructed, and thus there is no subject for collective action. The illusion of mobility (discussed further in Section 4.7) further dissolves identity recognition: if everyone believes that "as long as I work hard I can become a lord," no one will identify as a serf.

### 4.3 The Multiple Binding Constraints Structure

The crux of the trap lies in the four components forming a structure of **multiple binding constraints**. In economics, "multiple binding constraints" refers to a situation where multiple constraints bind simultaneously: when multiple constraints bind at once, relaxing any single one does not improve the outcome; only by relaxing sufficiently many constraints simultaneously will the system's state change. This is fundamentally different from a single-constraint situation (where relaxing that one constraint improves the outcome).

The relationship among the four components is not one of causal feedback ("breaking one causes another to re-close") but of simultaneously binding multiple constraints:

- Spatial dispersion constrains identity construction (without meeting, a "we" cannot form)
- Identity opacity constrains interest recognition (without identifying as serfs, shared interests cannot be recognized)
- The absence of trigger points constrains mobilization (without visible unjust events, there is no occasion for action)
- Infrastructure capture constrains organization (even if the first three were overcome, the tools of organization are in the hands of the target)

An online community connects dispersed annotation workers (relaxing the spatial dispersion constraint), but they do not see themselves as "exploited workers" (the identity opacity constraint still binds), so collective action still cannot be launched: not because spatial dispersion has "re-closed," but because another constraint remains effective. **The trap's power does not come from any single component's self-repair, but from the joint constraint formed by multiple constraints binding simultaneously.** This is more precise than "self-repair" and more amenable to empirical testing: if the trap is a structure of multiple binding constraints, then effective resistance must exert pressure on multiple constraints simultaneously, which is precisely the design logic behind the two levels of resistance strategies in Section V.

It should be noted that the "multiple binding constraints" structure partially exists in the gig economy as well (spatial dispersion and infrastructure capture apply to Uber drivers too). The specificity of AI annotation labor lies in this: the obscuring of employer visibility (outsourcing chains mean no single attributable employer) and the loss of leverage points (tasks can be globally reallocated). These two additional constraints make strategies that sometimes work in the gig economy (such as regional strikes, legislative pressure targeting a single platform) much harder to execute in AI annotation labor.

### 4.4 Empirical Corroboration of the Trap

This section is not pure theoretical deduction. All four components of the trap have observable empirical correlates, which together constitute a preliminary test of the trap hypothesis.

**Corroboration of spatial dispersion:** AI annotation workers, the bottom stratum of cognitive serfs in the current AI value chain, provide a clear test case for their organizational attempts. In May 2023, approximately **150** African workers supporting systems such as Facebook, TikTok, and ChatGPT gathered in Nairobi and pledged to establish the African Content Moderators Union (ACMU). This is the most significant organizational attempt in the AI annotation field to date, but 150 people, relative to millions of annotation workers globally, is an extremely small proportion. This proportion is itself corroboration of the spatial dispersion component: workers scattered across countries are exceedingly difficult to bring together into a single organizational action. For comparison: the unionization rate in the U.S. "Professional and Technical Services" industry (covering most tech employment) is only **1.3%**, surpassed only by finance. Even for formally employed U.S. tech workers, the organizational rate is already extremely low; the organizational difficulty for cross-border outsourced annotation workers is an order of magnitude higher.

**Corroboration of incorporation without trigger points:** The largest collective action attempt in the AI field to date is the "Pause Giant AI Experiments" open letter initiated by the Future of Life Institute in March 2023, which received over **30,000** signatures (including prominent researchers such as Bengio and Musk). **The actual effect was zero**: no major lab paused training. This case precisely demonstrates the third component of the trap: expressive collective action (signatures, appeals) lacks the leverage of traditional strikes (without halting production, no pressure can be applied); the moral appeal of 30,000 people could not constrain a capital-driven AI race. Most signatories were researchers rather than the dispossessed, further demonstrating that even relatively organized groups, without the capacity to halt production, cannot produce substantive impact.

**Corroboration of infrastructure capture and exit costs:** When annotation workers or developers attempt to migrate from one platform to another, they face costs that are not simply a matter of "switching accounts." Industry data shows that the average cost for an enterprise to migrate from one AI base to another is approximately **$315,000**, with switching costs accounting for 19-34% of total AI investment, and the sunk loss of fine-tuning assets can reach $100,000 to $500,000. OpenAI's API deprecation notice in November 2025 gave developers only about 6 months to migrate. This lock-in means that even if cognitive serfs wanted to collectively "vote with their feet," exit costs make large-scale migration nearly impossible. Each person is locked into their own platform assets, and the cost structure of collective switching makes coordinated action unformable.

**The gig economy as a control: what is specific to AI annotation labor.** The gig economy has already demonstrated the organizational difficulties of dispersed labor, difficulties that heavily overlap with the first two components of the CCT (spatial dispersion, infrastructure capture). The California Proposition 22 dispute is the key case: Uber, Lyft, and DoorDash spent over **$224 million** to pass Prop 22 (defining gig workers as independent contractors, excluding them from collective bargaining rights), making it the most expensive ballot measure in California history; in July 2024, the California Supreme Court unanimously upheld Prop 22.

**But the gig economy also shows the possibility of breakthroughs**. And this is precisely the key contrast the CCT must explain. In May 2026, approximately **70,000** Uber/Lyft drivers in Massachusetts formally established the first gig driver union with collective bargaining rights in the United States, covering over 800,000 drivers. If the first two components of the CCT (spatial dispersion + infrastructure capture) were sufficient to lock down collective action, Uber drivers should not have achieved this breakthrough. The reason they were able to break through is precisely that gig drivers are more favorably positioned than AI annotation workers on two dimensions related to the third and fourth CCT components:

First, **employer visibility.** Uber drivers face a single visible platform (Uber or Lyft); they know "who is exploiting me" and can point to a concrete negotiating counterparty. AI annotation workers face multiple layers of outsourcing chains: worker→contractor (e.g., Sama)→platform (e.g., Scale AI/Remotasks)→end client (e.g., OpenAI/Meta), no single "employer" can be pointed at, pressured, or negotiated with. The core difficulty of Daniel Motaung's 2022 lawsuit against Meta and Sama was precisely the ambiguity of the defendant's identity: who should be held responsible for the labor conditions of annotation workers?

Second, **leverage points.** Uber drivers have regional leverage: if drivers in one city collectively stop accepting rides, the city's ride-hailing service immediately collapses, exerting visible pressure on the platform. AI annotation workers' tasks can be globally reallocated by the platform: if Kenyan workers strike, the platform can transfer annotation tasks to the Philippines or Bangladesh without interrupting the production chain. The 2024 case of Remotasks' wholesale withdrawal from Kenya, Nigeria, and Pakistan demonstrates this even more starkly: the platform did not passively respond to worker strikes but proactively withdrew from the entire region, instantly nullifying the organizational networks workers had built within a country, and depriving workers of even the leverage to demand unpaid wages.

**These two dimensions, the obscuring of employer visibility and the loss of leverage points, constitute the specific constraints that distinguish AI annotation labor from the general gig economy.** They are not general features of platform labor (gig drivers are clearly more favorably positioned on both dimensions), but constraints directly tied to the specific structure of the means of cognitive production (globally allocatable annotation tasks, multi-layered intermediary outsourcing chains). The specificity of the CCT lies precisely here: it does not claim to have discovered all the mechanisms of organizational difficulty in platform labor (those have been systematically addressed in the work of Vallas & Schor, Prassl, and Cant), but rather identifies the specific intensification of AI annotation labor on the above two dimensions and integrates this into a multiple-binding-constraints framework.

### 4.5 The Falsifiability of the Trap

The value of a theoretical mechanism depends in part on whether it can be falsified. The key testable claim of the CCT is not a definitional proposition ("the AI field is more dispersed than factories," which is near-tautological) but a comparative empirical prediction:

**Prediction:** Controlling for per capita income, labor market size, and labor law frameworks, the unionization rate and collective action success rate of workers in AI annotation outsourcing hubs (such as Nairobi, Manila) should be **lower** than that of gig drivers (ride-hailing, delivery) in the same region, with the difference primarily attributable to two dimensions: employer visibility (outsourcing chain obscurity vs. single visible platform) and leverage points (globally allocatable vs. regional work stoppages).

This prediction is falsifiable: if research finds **no significant difference** between the unionization rates of AI annotation workers and gig drivers, then the CCT's "AI-specificity" claim is falsified, and the difficulty would be attributed to the general structure of platform labor (spatial dispersion + infrastructure capture) rather than the specific structure of the means of cognitive production. Currently, no systematic three-way comparative study exists using unionization rate as the dependent variable (AI annotation workers vs. gig drivers vs. traditional service workers). Existing data (ACMU with only 150 members, BLS tech industry unionization rate of 1.3%) provides directional support, but systematic comparative research remains to be conducted. One purpose of proposing the CCT concept in this paper is precisely to provide a testable theoretical framework for such empirical research.

### 4.6 The Organizational Impossibility of the Cognitively Excluded

If organizing cognitive serfs is already difficult (with all four components of the trap in effect), then organizing the cognitively excluded is nearly impossible: they cannot even reach the starting point of the trap.

The cognitively excluded are excluded precisely because they lack the conditions for participating in the cognitive production-consumption cycle (language, infrastructure, economic, literacy). And these are precisely the conditions necessary for organizing collective action. A person who cannot effectively use the internet cannot participate in online collective action; a person whose native language is not supported by any mainstream platform cannot be included in cross-language mobilization networks; a person struggling for basic survival has no surplus energy to participate in any form of organized action.

This constitutes a cruel paradox: **the group that most needs collective action (the cognitively excluded) is precisely the group least capable of being organized into collective action.** The conditions of their exclusion are simultaneously the conditions that make them unable to organize resistance. Structure and resistance capacity form a closed loop here: the structure deprives the excluded of the conditions for participation, and the deprivation of participation conditions in turn makes it impossible for the excluded to organize to change the structure.

This closed loop has no purely logical solution. It can only be partially, incrementally, and externally broken, by those not yet fully excluded (sympathizers among cognitive serfs, international NGOs, members of marginalized language communities who have already achieved some degree of digital access) fighting for visibility and conditions for the excluded. This is precisely the significance of the data contribution strategy in the first level of Section V.

### 4.7 Relationship Between the Trap and Other Protective Mechanisms of the Structure

The Cognitive Coordination Trap constitutes the **deepest protective layer** of the concentration structure of the means of cognitive production. The explicit protective mechanisms diagnosed in the previous paper are: institutional capture (distorting rules through lobbying), public discourse shaping (controlling narratives through media), and standard definition (rewriting evaluation through ecosystem position). The protective mechanisms identified in the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy are: hegemony (making people not question), exhaustion (making people have no energy), and coercion (making people not dare).

But beneath all these mechanisms lies the even more fundamental layer of the Cognitive Coordination Trap: **even if a dispossessed person questions the structure, has the energy to resist, and dares to resist, the coordination trap still makes them choose to wait.** No one needs to design this protective mechanism; it operates automatically as long as the dispossessed are each in a state of dispersion, without identity, without trigger points, and without autonomous infrastructure. Compute lords do not need to actively divide the dispossessed. The dispossessed are already unable to unite because of the trap's structure.

Two auxiliary narrative mechanisms further reinforce the trap. One is the **illusion of mobility**: the boundaries of the three-class structure appear fluid ("as long as you work hard you can become a lord"), which dismantles the cognitive basis of class solidarity, causing the dispossessed to direct blame at themselves rather than the structure. The other is the **narrative of technological inevitability**: "AI will naturally be dominated by a few large corporations because training frontier models requires enormous compute," which naturalizes a social structure as a technological law, making resistance appear irrational. The diagnosis of the previous paper has already refuted the factual basis of both narratives (compute concentration is not inevitable; the zero-copy-cost nature of weights means that openness has virtually zero technical cost), but the power of narratives lies not in their truth but in their credibility.

This analysis is consistent with the judgment of the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy, but with one important deepening: in traditional labor-capital relations, workers at least had the organizational foundation of physical concentration (the factory), and coordination dilemmas could be partially overcome by union organization. In the AI context, cognitive serfs lack even this foundation: the four components of the trap jointly eliminate the very possibility of organization. **This is the structural reason why the dispossessed in the AI era find it harder to resist than traditional workers, and it is the reason this paper formalizes it as an independent mechanism.**

## V. Two Levels of Resistance: From Within the Structure to Alternative Production Entities

Section IV formalized the Cognitive Coordination Trap, a structure of multiple binding constraints composed of spatial dispersion, infrastructure capture, incorporation without trigger points, and identity opacity. If the trap involves multiple binding constraints (where relaxing a single constraint is ineffective), then effective resistance cannot be a single strategy but must be multi-level. This section organizes resistance strategies into **two levels**, which differ in nature: the first level plays the game within the rules defined by compute lords (within-structure strategies), and the second level creates a production entity of a different nature (beyond-structure strategies). The first level has a ceiling; the second level attempts to break the ceiling.

### 5.1 Level One: Within-Structure Strategies—Contesting Limited Space

The following three strategies share a common feature: **they all operate within the framework of the means of cognitive production controlled by compute lords, using the cracks in the structure to secure limited space for the dispossessed.** They are not emancipatory plans but pragmatic strategies to improve conditions without changing the property rights structure.

**(I) Data contribution: breaking the invisibility of the cognitively excluded**

Target group: the cognitively excluded, especially low-resource language communities. The primary dilemma of the excluded is not "using poorly" but "not being seen at all." Their languages, cultures, and needs barely exist in training corpora. By participating in projects such as Common Voice (Mozilla's crowdsourced speech dataset), Masakhane (a collaborative African language NLP community), and OPUS (open parallel corpora), marginalized language communities can bring their languages into public training corpora. These contributions are slow, intergenerational, a language may require years of collective annotation work to move from "invisible" to "having basic representation." But it is the prerequisite for structural loosening: without presence in the data, any subsequent fine-tuning, adaptation, or services would be impossible.

Boundaries: This level depends on the organizational capacity and collective will of communities. It does not solve immediate service gaps. Another risk: if data contributions only enter public corpora, they may be used for free by compute lords (scraping public data to train closed-source models) without giving back to the contributing communities. Therefore, data contribution needs to be combined with community data governance (data cooperatives, usage condition agreements). But even without perfect data governance, existential contribution itself has value: it at least breaks the state of total invisibility.

**(II) Community fine-tuning: contesting adaptation on open-source bases**

Target group: communities whose languages or domains have some representation (though imperfect) in open-source base models. Through lightweight fine-tuning techniques such as LoRA, communities can adapt open-source bases to their own languages and domains. An open-source model that performs well in English but poorly in Swahili can be fine-tuned with community-organized annotation data to improve performance on specific tasks.

**But the limits of this strategy as resistance must be honestly acknowledged.** Not all fine-tuning activities on open-source bases constitute "resistance." Most fine-tuning on HuggingFace is actually developers building applications on Llama and publishing to commercial markets. This serves more to strengthen Llama's ecosystem stickiness for Meta than to challenge the structure of compute lords. The criterion distinguishing resistant fine-tuning from dependent fine-tuning is: the former fills service gaps that compute lords are unwilling to provide (low-resource languages, de-censored dialogue, cultural adaptation for specific communities); the latter increases the commercial ecosystem value of open-source bases (whose greatest beneficiaries are the large companies that trained the base). Only resistant fine-tuning constitutes a challenge to the structure.

Regardless of the type of fine-tuning, all face a common deep constraint: **as long as workers lack the capacity to train models from scratch, they must rely on a large company with that capacity to provide the base, and hope that this company maintains an open-source strategy.** This means that community fine-tuning (even when resistant) is always a dependent activity, whose preconditions are determined by the competitive strategies of big companies, not by community control. Big companies can change their open-source strategy at any time (the precedents of HashiCorp, Redis, and MongoDB shifting from open source to "source available" already exist), at which point all fine-tuning assets based on old versions face devaluation.

Empirical research also shows that the effectiveness of community fine-tuning is highly dependent on the quality of the base model's pre-training representations in the target language. For nearly invisible languages (where the base model has barely seen them during pre-training), fine-tuning only produces "more confident noise": LoRA cannot create non-existent representations out of thin air. Therefore, data contribution (I) and community fine-tuning (II) are complementary: data must first enable the base model to develop basic representations before fine-tuning becomes meaningful.

**(III) Serf competition: contesting portability among lords**

Target group: the more technically capable among cognitive serfs (developers, enterprise users, AI engineers). Not refusing to use lords' bases (that is a dead end, as argued in Section II), but exploiting competition among lords to force them to release more fine-tuning rights and ecosystem openness.

Mechanism: Lords compete for ecosystem dependence (developers, enterprise customers, data contributors). To attract this dependence, a particular lord may proactively open up more, similar to Google open-sourcing Android to counter Apple's vertical integration. Serfs, acting as "power users," can build portable fine-tuning toolchains (such as llama.cpp, Ollama, vLLM, and other unified inference frameworks) to make their fine-tuning assets transferable across different bases, thereby forcing more lords to follow suit with openness. The low-price open-source strategies of Chinese players such as DeepSeek, Alibaba's Tongyi Qianwen, and Zhipu have already exerted objective price and openness pressure on OpenAI and Anthropic, this is real-world corroboration of the logic of serf competition.

Boundaries: This strategy depends on a key premise, **that lords have not reached a tacit agreement of total closure.** If major compute lords form a jointly closed tacit agreement (jointly restricting fine-tuning, jointly raising migration costs, jointly resisting open standards), the foundation of this strategy would collapse. Currently, commercial competition tends toward some degree of openness, but this is a dynamic, reversible condition.

**The shared ceiling of Level One.** The three strategies above share a common structural limitation: **they all operate within the property rights framework defined by compute lords.** Data contributors' contributed data may be used for free by lords; community fine-tuners depend on bases released by lords; serf competitors depend on the competitive landscape among lords. No matter how clever the strategies, their efficacy ultimately depends on the strategic choices of compute lords, not on the dispossessed's own control. This is the insurmountable ceiling of "within-structure strategies": **without changing the property rights structure of the means of cognitive production, resistance can only contest space, not change the rules.**

### 5.2 Level Two: Alternative Production Entities—Beyond the Structure

The shared ceiling of Level One points toward a more fundamental strategic direction: **not playing the game on the bases of compute lords, but creating a production entity of a different nature: an AI infrastructure not driven by profit maximization, owned by the public or community, with returns flowing back to co-producers.** This is not contesting space within the structure, but attempting to change the structure itself.

**Why alternative production entities are needed.** The analysis of Level One has shown that as long as the property rights structure of the means of cognitive production remains unchanged, the strategic efficacy of the dispossessed is always constrained by the choices of compute lords. Data contribution, community fine-tuning, and serf competition are all valuable strategies, but their ceiling is structural. The only path to break this ceiling is: beyond the compute lords, establish a new cognitive production entity whose property rights structure prevents algorithmic rents from being monopolized and instead channels them back to co-producers. This is precisely the direction in which the $\rho_{ARA}$ (algorithmic rent allocation rate) proposed in the first paper needs to be fundamentally raised at the institutional level.

**Normative requirements.** A genuinely resistant alternative production entity must satisfy three normative conditions:

1. **Ownership attribution**: the entity is held by the public or community, not by private shareholders. This is different from "low price" or "open source": low price is a pricing parameter (which any competitor can temporarily adopt), open source is a licensing strategy (which any large company can choose or abandon), while ownership attribution is a fundamental arrangement of the property rights structure. An entity's "publicness" does not lie in whether it is low-priced (DeepSeek's low prices are a competitive strategy), but in who holds its residual claim, whether algorithmic rents flow to all owners (the public/community) or to a small number of shareholders. This is the property rights foundation of $\rho_{ARA}$.
2. **Democratic governance**: the entity is governed not by capital dictatorship (shareholders' meeting to board to management), but by the participation of diverse stakeholders, including owners (the public), technical experts, workers, and user representatives. The difficulty of governance lies in the tension between democratic participation and decision-making efficiency: the pace of AI frontier competition is extremely fast, and multi-stakeholder democratic processes may be slower than private competitors in decision speed. This tension cannot be resolved by abandoning democratic governance (which would fall back to traditional "administrative centralization" public ownership), but must be mitigated through honest positioning of technical goals (see "Honest Positioning of Technical Goals" below).
3. **Return flow**: the entity's operational returns (after reinvestment) do not flow to shareholders in the form of profits, but flow back to co-producers (the public, data contributors, annotation workers) in the form of universal dividends or public services. This elevates $\rho_{ARA}$ from near zero to a level significantly higher than that of private AI enterprises.

**Honest positioning of technical goals.** Alternative production entities do not need to win the AI arms race. An honest goal is **to catch up to the frontier rather than lead it**: to reduce training and service costs through efficiency innovation (rather than scale accumulation), to maintain "adequate and not lagging" capability in key areas, and to prioritize serving areas neglected by closed-source giants (low-resource languages, public service sectors, marginalized communities). DeepSeek has already demonstrated that through architectural innovation (MoE, multi-token prediction, etc.), "near-frontier" capability can be achieved at costs far below those of frontier model training. The goal of alternative production entities is not to replicate OpenAI, but to prove that **under a publicly owned property rights framework, cognitive production need not obey the logic of capital valorization.**

**Historical precedents.** Alternative production entities have referenceable historical precedents. Public broadcasting (BBC, NHK) provides public services beyond commercial media, changing the competitive nature of the media ecosystem. Public utilities (the universal service principle for electricity, water, and telecommunications) prevent private extraction in natural monopoly sectors through regulated or publicly owned forms. These precedents demonstrate that in sectors with strong economies of scale and public goods characteristics, the existence of public entities can change the competitive nature of the entire market. Not because public entities are always more efficient, but because their existence prevents private entities from extracting without restraint. AI infrastructure has similar economies of scale and public goods characteristics; the existence of alternative public AI entities can play the same counterbalancing function.

**Relationship with Level One.** Level Two does not replace Level One but provides Level One with a structural anchor. Data contributors' labor has a genuine home in alternative entities (data flows back to communities rather than being scraped for free by lords); community fine-tuners work on publicly owned bases provided by alternative entities (bases are publicly owned and will not suddenly change open-source strategy); serf competitors gain stronger negotiating leverage (the existence of alternative entities means that "exit" no longer means just another lord, but an entity of a different nature). Level One strategies gain a higher ceiling with the existence of Level Two.

**Conditions and boundaries.** The establishment of alternative production entities faces difficulties far greater than any Level One strategy. It requires political power to advance property rights restructuring (cannot be accomplished spontaneously by individuals or small communities), must address the risk of institutional capture (the managers of public entities may be lobbied or bought by compute lords; the anti-capture paradox of the third paper applies here as well), and must find a viable balance between democratic governance and technical efficiency. These difficulties are real. But the significance of alternative production entities lies not in their ease of realization, but in their pointing toward a fundamentally different direction: **not playing within the lords' game rules, but changing the game rules themselves.** The excessively low $\rho_{ARA}$ diagnosed in the first paper and the three-principle framework proposed in the third paper both require some institutional carrier for realization—alternative production entities are precisely the embryonic form of such a carrier. Their complete property rights and governance design (ownership lock-in, residual claim arrangements, multi-stakeholder checks and balances, anti-concentration mechanisms) constitutes an independent research topic beyond the scope of this paper.

## VI. Solidarity: The Common Foundation of Both Levels

The two levels of resistance in Section V (within-structure strategies and alternative production entities) differ in nature, but they share a common premise: **both require collective action.** Data contribution requires community collaboration, community fine-tuning requires collective annotation, serf competition requires the widespread adoption of standardized toolchains; alternative production entities require political power to advance property rights restructuring. Not a single resistance strategy can be realized through purely individual action. This is consistent with the conclusion of the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy: individual resistance is a dead end; structural change requires collective action. Collective action requires solidarity.

### 6.1 The Logical Necessity of Solidarity

Individuals cannot change structures. But individuals are not isolated: those dispossessed within the concentration structure of the means of cognitive production are not one person but millions upon millions. Dispersed, they have no power; but if they unite, they constitute a force within the structure capable of countering the institutional capture by compute lords.

This is the necessity of solidarity. Not a moral appeal, but a structural imperative: in a system that structurally deprives individuals of the capacity for resistance, the only possible lever is collective action. One person cannot refuse unjust rules; one million can. One person cannot make institutions respond; a social movement can.

This conclusion follows the logic of the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy. There, the objects of solidarity were the dispossessed in the traditional sense (workers, the poor, marginalized groups). In the AI context, the objects of solidarity expand to cognitive serfs and the cognitively excluded. The form of their "dispossession" differs (not wages being suppressed, but the means of cognitive production being monopolized), but the logical structure of solidarity is the same: **dispersed individuals are powerless to change structures; a united collective is the only force capable of countering the self-reinforcing cycle of institutional capture.**

### 6.2 The Special Difficulties of Solidarity in the AI Era (The Solidarity Dimension of the Trap)

But the Cognitive Coordination Trap of Section IV constitutes an important correction to any optimistic reading of the "necessity of solidarity." Solidarity is logically necessary but practically difficult, and the difficulty is itself another expression of the trap. The four components of the trap are not only obstacles to collective action but also obstacles to solidarity: spatial dispersion prevents the construction of trust networks, infrastructure capture places the organizational tools of solidarity in the hands of the target, incorporation without trigger points eliminates the emotional occasions for solidarity, and identity opacity makes the construction of "we" impossible.

Thus, the difficulty of solidarity in the AI era does not need to be re-argued: it is precisely the solidarity dimension of the trap. What truly needs to be asked is: **under the constraints of the trap, in what forms is solidarity possible?**

### 6.3 Possible Forms of Solidarity

Despite the weight of difficulties, solidarity is not impossible. Its possible forms may be non-traditional:

**Cross-stratum cognitive serf alliances.** There exist sufficient shared interests among highly skilled developers, enterprise users, and ordinary AI users (all are subject to compute lords' base control, API pricing, and ecosystem lock-in) that they may form some kind of alliance, contesting portability and bargaining power through standardized toolchains, open-source ecosystems, and industry self-regulation. Such alliances need not take the form of traditional unions; they may appear as "open-source communities + developer associations + enterprise alliances."

**International digital rights movements.** Similar to the internationalization of environmental movements or human rights, solidarity in the AI era may take the form of international digital rights movements: cross-border NGOs, academic networks, and policy advocacy coalitions pushing for institutional changes such as data rights legislation, algorithmic rent distribution, and low-resource language support.

**Global South and marginalized community alliances.** The cognitively excluded are highly concentrated in the Global South and marginalized language communities. Their solidarity may take the form of "Global South AI sovereignty," similar to historical resource sovereignty movements, Global South nations may jointly demand greater control over the means of cognitive production (especially public data and public compute). The BRICS New Development Bank, regional AI research collaborations, and multilingual model initiatives may be embryonic forms of such solidarity.

These forms are all preliminary, fragile, and uncertain. But their existence shows that solidarity in the AI era, though difficult, is not without the possibility of germination.

## VII. This Is Not Persuasion; This Is a Statement of Position

I am clearly aware that, at this point, this paper has moved from the immanent critique of mainstream frameworks in the previous paper to a place those frameworks cannot contain. The previous paper completed its diagnosis using the language of industrial organization economics, public choice theory, and Rawlsian distributive justice. Those tools were sharp enough to cut through the question of "whether the concentration of the means of cognitive production is just." But when it comes to "what is to be done," those tools fell silent. Liberal theories of action lose efficacy under deep capture, individual exit is a dead end, and the Cognitive Coordination Trap locks down the organizational conditions for collective action. The mainstream framework has nothing more to say.

The narrative of collective action and solidarity takes over from here. Not because it is more "correct," in an epistemological sense, no narrative can claim absolute correctness. But because, in the experience of history, it is the only intellectual tradition that has systematically developed the proposition of "how the oppressed change structures through collective action."

I am also aware that this section will not persuade anyone who does not already agree. Those who agree (those who have already felt the oppression of the concentration of the means of cognitive production) do not need this section to tell them that solidarity is necessary. Their experience of use has already taught them. Those who do not agree (those who benefit from the existing structure or believe that the existing structure can self-correct) will never be persuaded by this section, because they do not believe the structure needs to change.

The function of this section is not persuasion. It is a statement of position. What it says is: if you have followed the logic of this paper to this point (acknowledging the injustice of the phase transition of the means of cognitive production, acknowledging the systemic nature of institutional capture, acknowledging the dead end of individual exit, acknowledging the severity of the coordination dilemma), then you should not pretend that all of this has no implications for action by the dispossessed.

The end of the logic is here. The two levels of resistance strategies point to starting points of action for groups in different positions: within-structure strategies contest limited space, alternative production entities point toward changing the rules themselves. Institutional change requires solidarity, solidarity requires organization, organization requires action. This is not work that this paper can complete, but this paper can at least honestly point toward it.

## VIII. Conclusion: The Conditionality of Action

### 8.1 The Advancement of This Paper

The advancement of this paper can be condensed into a single sentence: in the AI era, individual resistance is a dead end, collective action faces the Cognitive Coordination Trap, a structure of multiple binding constraints, and the direction for breaking through the trap's ceiling is the creation of alternative production entities.

The argument supporting this judgment is: individual technological exit does not touch the structure (Section II), liberal theories of action lose efficacy under deep capture (Section III), and the Cognitive Coordination Trap causes the organizational conditions for collective action to be structurally eliminated (Section IV). The originality of the trap lies not in discovering the general organizational difficulties of platform labor (already addressed by Vallas & Schor, Prassl), but in identifying the specifically intensified constraints of AI annotation labor on two dimensions: employer visibility and leverage points. The contrast between the 70,000 Massachusetts Uber/Lyft drivers establishing a union in 2026 and the largest organizational attempt by AI annotation workers to date involving only 150 people provides preliminary support for the trap's falsifiable prediction.

Resistance strategies are therefore organized into two levels. Within-structure strategies (data contribution, community fine-tuning, serf competition) face an insurmountable ceiling: as long as the property rights structure remains unchanged, the efficacy of resistance is always constrained by the choices of compute lords. Alternative production entities point toward changing the property rights structure itself. The two levels are complementary, and their common premise is collective action and solidarity.

### 8.2 Relationship to the Second Paper of the Wealth Trilogy and Platform Labor Research

This paper is the AI-era counterpart of the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy, *The Legitimacy of Reclamation and the Necessity of Solidarity*. The two share a highly isomorphic argumentative structure: both move from diagnosis to action, both argue the dead end of individual action, the boundaries of liberalism, and the necessity of solidarity.

The advancement of this paper operates on two layers. The first layer (relative to the Wealth Trilogy): it formalizes the special difficulties of collective action in the AI era into a nameable structure of multiple binding constraints, the Cognitive Coordination Trap. The second paper of the Wealth Trilogy identified coordination dilemmas (Olson, Granovetter) but treated them as general dilemmas applicable to all dispossessed groups. This paper further argues that, in the AI context, this dilemma acquires a specific structural form. The second layer (relative to platform labor research): the CCT does not claim to have discovered all the mechanisms of organizational difficulty in platform labor, spatial dispersion and infrastructure capture have been systematically addressed in Vallas & Schor (2020), Prassl (2018), and Cant (2019). The incremental contribution of the CCT lies in identifying the specific constraints of AI annotation labor on two dimensions, employer visibility and leverage points, and integrating them into a multiple-binding-constraints framework. The significance of this two-layer positioning is that it prevents the CCT from either repeating existing literature (acknowledging inheritance) or dissolving its own contribution (identifying incremental advance), while simultaneously generating a falsifiable comparative prediction.

At the same time, this paper also faces an additional paradox absent from the second paper of the Wealth Trilogy: **the organizational impossibility of the cognitively excluded.** The group that most needs collective action is precisely the group least capable of being organized into collective action. This paradox has no purely logical solution; it can only be partially broken from the outside. This confers special importance on the data contribution strategy in Level One and on external solidarity actors (international NGOs, members of marginalized communities who have already achieved digital access), and also gives special significance to Level Two's alternative production entities (which can provide the excluded with public AI services that do not require being "organized first" to benefit from).

### 8.3 The Honesty of Action

The honesty of action demands acknowledgment that Level One strategies (data contribution, community fine-tuning, serf competition) are not solutions; they are pragmatic strategies for contesting limited space within the structure. Their ceiling is structural: as long as the property rights structure of the means of cognitive production remains unchanged, the efficacy of these strategies is always constrained by the choices of compute lords. Level Two (alternative production entities) points toward a more fundamental direction, but its realization requires political power to advance property rights restructuring, and its feasibility faces severe challenges.

But the honesty of action also demands acknowledgment of the other side: **inaction deterministically preserves the status quo.** The concentration of the means of cognitive production will not dissolve on its own just because it has been diagnosed; the three-class structure will not loosen automatically just because it has been identified; $\rho_{ARA}$ will not rise automatically just because it has been proposed; the Cognitive Coordination Trap will not unlock on its own just because it has been named. All change requires the practice of actors, starting from the strategy in Level One closest to one's own position, while accumulating conditions and organizational experience for the institutional changes of Level Two.

What this paper can do is point toward a direction for such practice: **begin by recognizing one's own structural position, contest space within the structure, while accumulating political conditions and organizational capacity for the alternative production entities that transcend the structure.** The path out of the conditional dilemma must be walked by the actors themselves. As for how to design new institutions that will not be captured after walking out. That is the question for the third paper.
